# Anger
Anger is a normal emotional response, but when frequent or intense, it can become harmful. It may stem from frustration, injustice, or trauma.

How to manage:
- Practice deep breathing and mindfulness
- Identify triggers and avoid escalation
- Use grounding techniques
- Seek therapy such as CBT or anger management groups

Common medications:
- SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine) if linked to anxiety/depression

---

# Anxiety and Panic Attacks
Anxiety is persistent worry or fear; panic attacks are sudden episodes of intense fear with physical symptoms like rapid heartbeat.

How to manage:
- Breathing exercises and grounding
- CBT and exposure therapy
- Regular exercise and reduced caffeine
- Mindfulness meditation

Common medications:
- SSRIs (e.g., sertraline)
- Benzodiazepines (short-term use)
- Beta-blockers (for physical symptoms)

---

# Bipolar Disorder
Characterized by episodes of mania (high energy, impulsivity) and depression (low mood, fatigue).

How to manage:
- Consistent routine and sleep
- Psychoeducation and mood tracking
- Therapy (CBT, family-focused)

Common medications:
- Mood stabilizers (e.g., lithium)
- Antipsychotics (e.g., quetiapine)
- Antidepressants (with caution)

---

# Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD)
Involves obsessive focus on perceived flaws in appearance, often minor or imagined.

How to manage:
- CBT focused on body image
- Avoidance reduction strategies
- Exposure and response prevention (ERP)

Common medications:
- SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine, fluvoxamine)

---

# Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)
Involves emotional instability, impulsivity, and intense fear of abandonment.

How to manage:
- Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)
- Emotion regulation and distress tolerance skills
- Supportive relationships and crisis planning

Common medications:
- Mood stabilizers (e.g., lamotrigine)
- Antidepressants (if co-occurring depression)
- Antipsychotics (e.g., aripiprazole)

---

# Depression
A mood disorder marked by persistent sadness, loss of interest, fatigue, and feelings of worthlessness.

How to manage:
- CBT and behavioral activation
- Exercise and structured routine
- Social support and self-care

Common medications:
- SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine, citalopram)
- SNRIs (e.g., venlafaxine)
- Atypical antidepressants (e.g., bupropion)

---

# Eating Problems
Include anorexia, bulimia, and binge eating, often tied to control, body image, and emotional regulation.

How to manage:
- Nutritional therapy and CBT-E
- Support groups and family therapy
- Addressing underlying trauma or anxiety

Common medications:
- SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine for bulimia)
- Antipsychotics (e.g., olanzapine for anorexia)

---

# Hoarding
Persistent difficulty discarding items, resulting in clutter that affects living space and safety.

How to manage:
- CBT with exposure to discarding
- Decluttering plans with support
- Avoidance of collecting triggers

Common medications:
- SSRIs (e.g., paroxetine)

---

# Hypomania and Mania
Elevated mood states marked by increased energy, reduced need for sleep, and impulsive behavior (mania is more severe).

How to manage:
- Sleep regulation
- Stress reduction and structured routine
- Psychoeducation

Common medications:
- Mood stabilizers (e.g., lithium, valproate)
- Antipsychotics (e.g., risperidone)

---

# Loneliness
Feeling disconnected or lacking meaningful social interaction, which can affect physical and mental health.

How to manage:
- Join support groups or clubs
- Practice self-compassion
- Engage in volunteering or social activities

Common medications:
- Not typically medicated unless related to depression/anxiety

---

# Mental Health Problems - Introduction
Mental health problems can affect thoughts, emotions, behaviors, and daily functioning. Seeking support early can lead to better outcomes.

---

# Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)
Involves unwanted intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and repetitive behaviors (compulsions) aimed at reducing distress.

How to manage:
- CBT with Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP)
- Mindfulness to reduce compulsive urges

Common medications:
- SSRIs (e.g., fluvoxamine, sertraline)

---

# Paranoia
Excessive suspicion and mistrust of others, not based on evidence, sometimes linked to psychosis or personality disorders.

How to manage:
- Build trust with a therapist
- Practice reality testing
- Use grounding techniques

Common medications:
- Antipsychotics (e.g., olanzapine, risperidone)

---

# Personality Disorder
Long-term patterns of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that deviate from cultural expectations and cause distress.

How to manage:
- Schema therapy or DBT
- Long-term psychotherapy
- Psychoeducation

Common medications:
- Medications used based on symptoms (e.g., mood stabilizers, antidepressants)

---

# Phobias
Intense fear of a specific object, situation, or activity that is out of proportion to the actual danger.

How to manage:
- Exposure therapy (gradual desensitization)
- CBT to challenge distorted beliefs

Common medications:
- Beta-blockers (for physical symptoms)
- SSRIs for social phobia

---

# Postnatal Depression & Perinatal Mental Health
Depression during pregnancy or after childbirth, often accompanied by anxiety or guilt.

How to manage:
- Peer support and parent groups
- CBT or interpersonal therapy
- Partner/family involvement

Common medications:
- SSRIs (e.g., sertraline — considered safe in breastfeeding)

---

# Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)
Develops after exposure to a traumatic event, involving flashbacks, nightmares, and hypervigilance.

How to manage:
- Trauma-focused CBT or EMDR
- Grounding and relaxation techniques

Common medications:
- SSRIs (e.g., paroxetine, sertraline)
- Prazosin (for nightmares)

---

# Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD)
A severe form of PMS causing mood swings, irritability, and depression before menstruation.

How to manage:
- Lifestyle changes and tracking symptoms
- CBT for emotional regulation

Common medications:
- SSRIs during luteal phase (e.g., fluoxetine)
- Hormonal treatments (e.g., oral contraceptives)

---

# Psychosis
Loss of contact with reality, including hallucinations or delusions.

How to manage:
- Early intervention
- CBT for psychosis
- Psychoeducation and family therapy

Common medications:
- Antipsychotics (e.g., risperidone, aripiprazole)

---

# Recreational Drugs, Alcohol, and Addiction
Substance misuse can cause or worsen mental health problems and impact mood, sleep, and cognition.

How to manage:
- Detox and rehabilitation programs
- Behavioral therapies (CBT, motivational interviewing)
- Peer support (e.g., NA, AA)

Common medications:
- Naltrexone, methadone, disulfiram (based on substance)

---

# Schizoaffective Disorder
Features symptoms of both schizophrenia (psychosis) and mood disorder .

How to manage:
- Regular medication
- CBT and supportive therapy
- Lifestyle stabilization

Common medications:
- Antipsychotics + mood stabilizers or antidepressants

---

# Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)
Depression that occurs seasonally, usually in winter.

How to manage:
- Light therapy (10,000 lux lamp)
- Regular sleep and outdoor exposure
- CBT for mood

Common medications:
- SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine)

---

# Self-Esteem
Low self-esteem affects how you view yourself and can lead to anxiety, depression, or social withdrawal.

How to manage:
- Positive self-talk and affirmations
- Self-compassion practices
- Therapy (CBT, ACT)

Common medications:
- Not treated with medication unless symptoms link to depression/anxiety

---

# Self-Harm
Deliberate injury to oneself as a coping mechanism for emotional distress.

How to manage:
- Identify triggers and use alternative coping tools
- Keep a safety plan
- Seek professional support (CBT, DBT)

Common medications:
- Treat underlying conditions (e.g., antidepressants)

---

# Sleep Problems
Includes insomnia, nightmares, poor sleep hygiene, and restless sleep.

How to manage:
- Consistent sleep schedule
- Avoid screens before bed
- Sleep hygiene techniques

Common medications:
- Melatonin (OTC)
- Prescription sleep aids (short-term use)
- Antidepressants (if related to anxiety/depression)

---

# Stress
A mental or emotional strain resulting from demanding circumstances, often linked to work or personal life.

How to manage:
- Time management and relaxation techniques
- Exercise and hobbies
- Mindfulness and CBT

Common medications:
- Not directly medicated; SSRIs may help if anxiety develops

---

# Suicidal Feelings
Intense thoughts about ending one's life. It's a crisis situation that needs immediate support.

How to manage:
- Talk to a trusted person or call a crisis line
- Avoid being alone — seek immediate help
- Crisis counseling or inpatient support

Common medications:
- Treat underlying conditions (e.g., depression)

---

# Suicide Attempt
An act of trying to end one’s life. Urgent medical and psychological support is needed.

How to manage:
- Hospital stabilization
- Intensive therapy and follow-up care
- Safety plans and 24/7 support access

Common medications:
- Antidepressants, mood stabilizers, antipsychotics as needed

---

# Tardive Dyskinesia
A movement disorder caused by long-term use of antipsychotics, involving repetitive, involuntary movements.

How to manage:
- Reduce or adjust medication under supervision
- Use newer antipsychotics (e.g., clozapine)

Common medications:
- VMAT2 inhibitors (e.g., valbenazine)

---

# Trauma
Emotional or psychological response to a distressing event (e.g., abuse, accidents, disaster).

How to manage:
- Trauma-focused CBT or EMDR
- Build safety and trust before processing trauma
- Use grounding during flashbacks

Common medications:
- SSRIs or SNRIs

# Topic: Depression

Depression is a mental health problem that involves having a low mood or losing interest and enjoyment in things. It can also cause a range of other changes to how you feel or behave.

The symptoms you experience may vary. How intense they are, how long they last, and how much they affect your daily life can also vary.

If you experience milder depression, you might have low mood but still be able to carry on with your daily life. But things may feel harder and less worthwhile.

If you have more severe depression, you might find day-to-day life much more difficult. You may also experience suicidal feelings.

## Are there different types of depression?
- Persistent depressive disorder (PDD)
- Seasonal affective disorder (SAD)
- Antenatal depression
- Postnatal depression (PND)
- Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD)

## Common signs and symptoms of depression

### How you might feel:
- Down, upset or tearful
- Restless, agitated or irritable
- Guilty, worthless and down on yourself
- Empty and numb
- Isolated and unable to relate to other people
- Finding no pleasure in life or things you usually enjoy
- Angry or frustrated over minor things
- A sense of unreality
- No self-confidence or self-esteem
- Hopeless and despairing
- Feeling tired all the time

### How you might act:
- Avoiding social events and activities you usually enjoy
- Self-harming or suicidal behaviour
- Difficulty speaking, thinking clearly or making decisions
- Losing interest in sex
- Difficulty remembering or concentrating on things
- Using more tobacco, alcohol or other drugs than usual
- Difficulty sleeping, or sleeping too much
- No appetite and losing weight, or eating more than usual and gaining weight
- Physical aches and pains with no obvious physical cause
- Moving very slowly, or being restless and agitated

## Self-care for depression
- Talk to someone you trust
- Try peer support or join an online support group
- Visit a recovery college
- Try mindfulness or meditation
- Take care of yourself: eat, sleep, and move regularly
- Spend time in nature
- Try something new: hobbies, volunteering, creativity
- Keep a mood diary or write in a journal
- Make a self-care box

## Treatments for depression

### Self-help resources:
- Guided self-help programs
- Physical activity programs

### Talking therapies:
- Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)
- Group CBT or BA
- Mindfulness-based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT)
- Interpersonal Psychotherapy (IPT)
- Counselling or short-term psychodynamic psychotherapy
- Problem-solving or behavioural couples therapy

### Medication:
- SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine, sertraline)
- SNRIs, tricyclics, MAOIs
- Lithium (if severe or recurring)
- Antipsychotics (if psychotic symptoms present)

### Other treatments:
- Arts and creative therapies
- Complementary therapies (e.g., mindfulness, ecotherapy)
- Peer support
- Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) for severe cases
- Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS)
- Neurosurgery or deep brain stimulation (rare)

## Supporting someone with depression
- Listen without judgment
- Keep in touch and check in regularly
- Help with practical tasks
- Respect their pace and boundaries
- Take care of your own wellbeing

## Emergency support
If someone is in immediate danger:
- Call 999 or take them to A&E
- Call NHS 111 (option 2)
- Contact Samaritans at 116 123

# Topic: ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder)

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental condition involving differences in how the brain works. It is not a mental health condition or a learning disability, but can affect mental health. 

## Types of ADHD:
1. Inattentiveness and distractibility – trouble focusing
2. Hyperactivity and impulsivity – feeling overly active and impulsive
3. Combined type – mix of both traits

ADHD typically begins in childhood but may be diagnosed at any age. It can present differently based on gender, age, and comorbid conditions.

## Getting help for ADHD:
- Start with a visit to a GP
- GP may refer to a psychiatrist or mental health services for an assessment
- Assessment involves questions about symptoms, possibly interviews with family/teachers

## Treatment:
- Medication (stimulants and non-stimulants)
- Counselling and therapy (CBT, behavior therapy)
- Support groups

## ADHD and Mental Health:
People with ADHD may also experience anxiety, depression, or oppositional defiant disorder (ODD).

## Tips for Managing ADHD:
- Seek support at school or university
- Exercise regularly
- Talk to a friend or join peer support groups
- Take breaks during tasks
- Use planning tools and reminders

# Topic: Anorexia Nervosa

Anorexia is a serious eating disorder where individuals eat very little to maintain low body weight, often due to distorted body image and low self-esteem.

## Emotional and Behavioral Signs:
- Calorie counting and strict eating rules
- Skipping meals or excessive exercise
- Panic about eating in public
- Preoccupation with weight and body image
- Low mood, irritability, and disinterest in hobbies

## Physical Signs:
- Rapid weight loss
- Hormonal changes (e.g., irregular periods)
- Poor circulation, always feeling cold
- Downy hair growth, bloating, constipation
- Poor sleep and concentration

## Treatment:
- Medical assessment and nutritional rehabilitation
- Therapy (CBT, group and family therapy)
- Support from dietitians and mental health professionals
- Hospitalization in severe cases

## Supporting Someone with Anorexia:
- Listen non-judgmentally and reassure them
- Validate their feelings and show patience
- Ask how you can support them (appointments, resources)
- Encourage them to talk to a trusted adult or professional
- Stay in touch and involve them in non-triggering activities
- Avoid conversations about weight, body, or diets

With the right help, recovery is possible.

# Topic: Managing Anxiety
- Focus on your breathing: Inhale slowly for 4 seconds, hold for 4, exhale for 6. Repeat several times.
- Try grounding: Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste.
- Limit caffeine and sugar — these can heighten anxious feelings.
- Journaling your thoughts helps externalize and process anxiety.

# Topic: Coping with Depression
- Establish a daily routine — even simple structure helps.
- Avoid isolation: Talk to someone you trust or spend time in safe, social settings.
- Set small, achievable goals each day.
- Practice self-compassion. Healing is not linear.

# Topic: Self-Care Practices
- Prioritize sleep — aim for 7–9 hours and keep a consistent bedtime.
- Move your body daily — even a walk outside can improve mood.
- Hydrate and nourish your body with balanced meals.
- Schedule "you time" — listen to music, read, or just rest.

# Topic: Emotional Grounding Techniques
- Use temperature: Hold ice cubes or splash cold water on your face.
- Carry a calming object (stone, soft cloth, etc.) in your pocket.
- Repeat an affirmation: “I am safe. I am in control. I will be okay.”
- Visualize a safe place — imagine the sights, sounds, and smells.

# Topic: Coping with Burnout
- Take regular breaks, even 5 minutes each hour.
- Disconnect from screens and devices for rest.
- Ask for help — you don’t have to do everything alone.
- Remind yourself: Rest is productive.

# Topic: Suicide Prevention and Crisis Support
- You are not alone — many people care and want to help.
- If you're in crisis, talk to a trusted person immediately.
- In the U.S., call 988 — the Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (24/7).
- If you feel unsafe, seek emergency support or call local authorities.

# Topic: Positive Affirmations
- “I am doing the best I can.”
- “My feelings are valid, and they will pass.”
- “I deserve kindness, including from myself.”
- “Every step forward, no matter how small, is progress.”

# Topic: When to Seek Professional Help
- You feel persistently hopeless for more than two weeks.
- You’ve lost interest in things you used to enjoy.
- You experience intrusive or distressing thoughts.
- You have thoughts of self-harm, suicide, or feel unsafe.

# Topic: Social Anxiety Tips
- Prepare short responses for common questions (“What do you do?”, etc.)
- Focus on the other person — ask questions and listen.
- It's okay to excuse yourself if overwhelmed.
- Social skills improve with practice, not perfection.

# Topic: Mindfulness Techniques
- Body scan meditation: Focus on each part of your body from head to toe.
- Observe your thoughts without judgment — let them float by like clouds.
- Use your senses: Listen to surrounding sounds with full attention.
- Try mindful walking — pay attention to each step and breath.
